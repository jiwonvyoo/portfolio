Introduction

Dropdown menus are an essential part of user interfaces, yet their accessibility is often overlooked. This case study explores how dropdown menus in various applications incorporate accessibility features and how they can be improved. The goal is to analyze existing designs, identify areas for enhancement, and propose a redesigned dropdown menu that improves usability, efficiency, and accessibility.

Part 3: Redesign

State models were developed to visualize user interaction paths for both mouse and keyboard users.

I also explored a tradeoff scenario by removing hover/focus states to simplify screen reader interpretation. This helped highlight the tension between clarity and simplicity for different users.

Finally, I designed an improved dropdown integrating stronger visual feedback and keyboard affordances for broader accessibility.

Part 4: Reflection

The dropdown menus in existing applications generally support basic usability but often lack strong accessibility considerations. While they offer fundamental focus states, they often fail to provide sufficient clarity for users with disabilities.

My redesign strengthens accessibility with bold highlights, larger spacing, and clear focus indicators. These adjustments make the component more navigable for users with vision or motor impairments.

Kat Holmes’ idea of solving mismatches shaped my approach. Interfaces that don't consider varied user needs inherently exclude many. Spacing, affordances, and input compatibility are essential to address those mismatches.

One positive impact is intuitive keyboard control—using arrow keys and Enter to select—helping those who rely solely on keyboards. A potential drawback, however, is that removing hover states (in the tradeoff design) could confuse users who depend on visual cues for selection.

Most dropdowns are optimized for mouse use, making them less inclusive. My redesign prioritizes equal usability across input types to create a balanced experience.
