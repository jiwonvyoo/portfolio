Introduction

The RIPTA (Rhode Island Public Transit Authority) is a public transportation accessible to all Brown University students with their student ID. This study explores the usability of this system, focusing on how users (Brown University Students) interact with the swipe mechanism, common difficulties encountered, and potential improvements to enhance accessibility and efficiency.

Part 1: Preparation

The study is conducted through direct observation and user interviews to analyze the efficiency of the swipe interface. The key focus is to understand usability barriers and how users respond to system feedback.

Interface Selection

The RIPTA card swipe interface allows passengers to pay their fare using a swipe-based card system. The primary components include:

  • A card slot for swiping student IDs.
  • An audio beep for successful swipes.
  • A green screen that shows success text when transaction is made.
  • A "Card Not Valid" error sound, but no other error messages.

The system eliminates the need for cash but presents usability challenges such as unclear swipe direction, lack of error-specific feedback, and inconsistent card detection.

Interview Questions

  1. Can you describe your experience using the RIPTA card swipe interface?
  2. What do you find most intuitive about the interface?
  3. Have you ever encountered difficulties while using the interface?
  4. How does this interface compare to other public transit payment systems?
  5. What improvements would you suggest to make the interface more user-friendly?
  6. Have you observed other passengers struggling with the interface?
  7. How do you feel about the feedback provided by the interface?

Part 2: Recording Observations

Key Observations

  • Users often swiped cards multiple times before successful recognition.
  • Swipe direction was unclear, leading to hesitation and errors.
  • Error messages lacked specificity, creating uncertainty.
  • Passengers with bags struggled to swipe due to height/angle placement.

Key Findings from User Interviews

  • Directionality Issues: Unclear or faded instructions create multiple failed attempts.
  • Reliability Concerns: Cards often fail to register; feedback is insufficient.
  • Feedback Limitations: Screen/audio prompts are minimal and easy to miss.
  • Improvement Suggestions: Contactless payment, clearer visual/audio feedback, repositioned card reader.

Part 3: Personas

Person A: Frustrated Commuter

Relies on RIPTA weekly but finds the swipe system unreliable and confusing.

  • Swipe errors common, no clarity on cause.
  • Wants quicker, more reliable access.

Person B: New User

New to the system, experiences anxiety and confusion using the interface.

  • Unclear signage increases first-time errors.
  • Feedback is vague, causing uncertainty.

Part 4: Storyboarding

This storyboard illustrates the first-time user experience and common pain points.

  • Confusion around swipe direction and feedback clarity.
  • Anxiety from delays and lack of confirmation.

Reflection

This project deepened my understanding of how even the simplest public interfaces—like a card swipe reader—can have profound impacts on user experience, particularly for first-time or frequent users. While the system technically fulfills its purpose, the lack of clear feedback and intuitive guidance creates unnecessary barriers and frustration. Conducting interviews and observations highlighted the gap between system design and user expectations, reinforcing the importance of accessibility, feedback clarity, and affordances in interface design.

Moreover, creating personas and storyboards allowed me to empathize more deeply with different user types. I learned that good design not only solves functional problems but also reduces anxiety, increases confidence, and fosters inclusivity. This study reminded me that every interface, no matter how minor, should be designed with the user’s emotional and physical context in mind.
